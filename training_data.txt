# SECTION 1: FOUNDATIONAL EQUATIONS OF QUANTUM MECHANICS

## The Schrödinger Equation

Question: What is the Schrödinger equation and how does it govern quantum systems?

Answer: The Schrödinger equation is the fundamental dynamical equation of non-relativistic quantum mechanics, playing the same role that Newton's second law F=ma plays in classical mechanics. It describes how the quantum state of a physical system evolves in time.

The time-dependent Schrödinger equation for a single particle is:

    iℏ ∂ψ(x,t)/∂t = Ĥψ(x,t)

where:
- i is the imaginary unit (√-1)
- ℏ = h/(2π) = 1.054571817 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s is the reduced Planck constant
- ψ(x,t) is the complex wavefunction
- Ĥ is the Hamiltonian operator

For a particle of mass m in a potential V(x), the Hamiltonian is:

    Ĥ = -ℏ²/(2m) ∇² + V(x)

The first term is the kinetic energy operator and the second is the potential energy. The Laplacian ∇² in Cartesian coordinates is:

    ∇² = ∂²/∂x² + ∂²/∂y² + ∂²/∂z²

For stationary states with definite energy E, we separate variables ψ(x,t) = φ(x)e^(-iEt/ℏ) to obtain the time-independent Schrödinger equation:

    Ĥφ(x) = Eφ(x)

This is an eigenvalue equation where E is the energy eigenvalue and φ(x) is the corresponding eigenstate. The allowed values of E are typically quantized.

## The Schrödinger Equation in One Dimension

Question: How do we solve the Schrödinger equation for simple one-dimensional systems?

Answer: For a one-dimensional system with potential V(x), the time-independent Schrödinger equation becomes:

    -ℏ²/(2m) d²ψ/dx² + V(x)ψ(x) = Eψ(x)

This is a second-order ordinary differential equation. The solutions depend critically on the form of V(x).

Example: Free Particle (V=0)
The equation becomes:
    d²ψ/dx² = -(2mE/ℏ²)ψ

Defining k² = 2mE/ℏ², the general solution is:
    ψ(x) = Ae^(ikx) + Be^(-ikx)

These are plane waves with momentum p = ℏk and energy E = ℏ²k²/(2m) = p²/(2m).

Example: Infinite Square Well (Particle in a Box)
For V(x) = 0 inside [0,L] and V = ∞ outside, the boundary conditions ψ(0) = ψ(L) = 0 give:

    ψₙ(x) = √(2/L) sin(nπx/L)
    Eₙ = n²π²ℏ²/(2mL²) = n² × E₁

where n = 1, 2, 3, ... The ground state (n=1) has nonzero energy E₁ = π²ℏ²/(2mL²), demonstrating zero-point energy.

## The Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

Question: What is Heisenberg's uncertainty principle and what are its precise mathematical bounds?

Answer: The Heisenberg uncertainty principle is a fundamental limit on the precision with which certain pairs of physical observables can be simultaneously determined. It is NOT a statement about measurement disturbance, but a fundamental property of quantum states.

For position x and momentum p, the uncertainty relation is:

    Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2

where Δx and Δp are the standard deviations:
    Δx = √(⟨x²⟩ - ⟨x⟩²)
    Δp = √(⟨p²⟩ - ⟨p⟩²)

The value ℏ/2 ≈ 5.27 × 10⁻³⁵ J·s is the minimum uncertainty product, achieved only by Gaussian wavepackets (minimum uncertainty states).

The general uncertainty relation for any two observables A and B is:

    ΔA · ΔB ≥ ½|⟨[Â,B̂]⟩|

where [Â,B̂] = ÂB̂ - B̂Â is the commutator. For x and p:
    [x̂,p̂] = iℏ

Since the right side is iℏ (purely imaginary), |⟨iℏ⟩| = ℏ, giving Δx·Δp ≥ ℏ/2.

Other uncertainty relations include:
- Energy-time: ΔE · Δt ≥ ℏ/2
- Angular momentum: ΔLₓ · ΔLᵧ ≥ (ℏ/2)|⟨Lᵤ⟩|
- Spin-1/2: ΔSₓ · ΔSᵧ ≥ (ℏ/2)|⟨Sᵤ⟩|

## The Born Rule and Probability

Question: What is the Born rule and how does it connect wavefunctions to experimental measurements?

Answer: The Born rule, proposed by Max Born in 1926, provides the fundamental link between the mathematical formalism of quantum mechanics and experimental observations. It states that the wavefunction ψ(x) determines the probability of measurement outcomes.

For a position measurement, the probability density is:

    P(x) = |ψ(x)|² = ψ*(x)ψ(x)

where ψ*(x) is the complex conjugate of ψ(x). The probability of finding the particle in the interval [a,b] is:

    P(a ≤ x ≤ b) = ∫ₐᵇ |ψ(x)|² dx

Normalization requires:
    ∫₋∞^∞ |ψ(x)|² dx = 1

This means the total probability of finding the particle somewhere must equal 1.

For discrete observables with eigenvalues {aₙ} and eigenstates {|aₙ⟩}, if the system is in state |ψ⟩, the probability of measuring value aₙ is:

    P(aₙ) = |⟨aₙ|ψ⟩|²

The expectation value (average over many measurements) is:
    ⟨Â⟩ = ⟨ψ|Â|ψ⟩ = Σₙ aₙ|⟨aₙ|ψ⟩|²

Key insight: The Born rule is why quantum mechanics is inherently probabilistic. Even with complete knowledge of the wavefunction, we can only predict probabilities, not definite outcomes.

## Commutators and the Position-Momentum Relation

Question: What are commutators and why is [x̂,p̂] = iℏ so important?

Answer: The commutator of two operators Â and B̂ measures the extent to which their operations fail to commute:

    [Â,B̂] = ÂB̂ - B̂Â

If [Â,B̂] = 0, the operators commute and can share simultaneous eigenstates. If [Â,B̂] ≠ 0, there is a fundamental incompatibility.

The canonical commutation relation is:

    [x̂,p̂] = iℏ

This can be verified using the position representation where x̂ → x (multiplication) and p̂ → -iℏ∂/∂x:

    [x̂,p̂]ψ = x(-iℏ∂ψ/∂x) - (-iℏ∂/∂x)(xψ)
            = -iℏx(∂ψ/∂x) + iℏ(∂/∂x)(xψ)
            = -iℏx(∂ψ/∂x) + iℏψ + iℏx(∂ψ/∂x)
            = iℏψ

Therefore [x̂,p̂] = iℏ (the identity operator times iℏ).

Consequences of this fundamental relation:
1. Position and momentum cannot be simultaneously measured exactly (uncertainty principle)
2. Translations in position are generated by momentum: e^(-iâp̂/ℏ)|x⟩ = |x+a⟩
3. The energy-time uncertainty relation ΔE·Δt ≥ ℏ/2 follows from time translations
4. This is the quantum analog of the classical Poisson bracket {x,p} = 1

Important commutators:
- [Lᵢ,Lⱼ] = iℏεᵢⱼₖLₖ (angular momentum)
- [â,â†] = 1 (creation/annihilation operators)
- [H,p] = 0 for free particles (momentum conserved)

## Wavefunction Collapse and Measurement

Question: What happens to the wavefunction when a measurement is made?

Answer: According to the Copenhagen interpretation, measurement causes wavefunction collapse (or reduction). Before measurement, a system can exist in a superposition of eigenstates. Upon measurement, the wavefunction instantaneously collapses to the eigenstate corresponding to the measured value.

If the initial state is:
    |ψ⟩ = Σₙ cₙ|aₙ⟩

where |aₙ⟩ are eigenstates of observable A with eigenvalues aₙ, then:
- Probability of measuring aₙ: P(aₙ) = |cₙ|²
- State after measuring aₙ: |ψ⟩ → |aₙ⟩

The collapse postulate is separate from Schrödinger evolution:
- Between measurements: |ψ(t)⟩ = e^(-iĤt/ℏ)|ψ(0)⟩ (unitary, deterministic)
- At measurement: |ψ⟩ → |aₙ⟩ (non-unitary, probabilistic)

This leads to the measurement problem: Why does measurement cause collapse while unitary evolution is smooth? Interpretations vary:
- Copenhagen: Collapse is a fundamental process
- Many-worlds: No collapse; all outcomes occur in branching universes  
- Decoherence: Effective collapse from environment entanglement
- Objective collapse: Physical process (GRW, Penrose)

Repeated measurements: If we immediately remeasure A after obtaining aₙ, we always get aₙ again (the state is already |aₙ⟩). This is the eigenstate-eigenvalue link.

## Hydrogen Atom Energy Levels

Question: What are the energy levels of the hydrogen atom?

Answer: The hydrogen atom consists of one proton (nucleus) and one electron bound by the Coulomb potential:

    V(r) = -e²/(4πε₀r) = -ke²/r

where k = 1/(4πε₀) ≈ 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C². Solving the Schrödinger equation in spherical coordinates gives the energy eigenvalues:

    Eₙ = -13.6 eV / n²

where n = 1, 2, 3, ... is the principal quantum number.

CORRECT ENERGY VALUES:
| n | Energy (eV) | Energy (J)           |
|---|-------------|----------------------|
| 1 | -13.6       | -2.18 × 10⁻¹⁸       |
| 2 | -3.40       | -5.45 × 10⁻¹⁹       |
| 3 | -1.51       | -2.42 × 10⁻¹⁹       |
| 4 | -0.85       | -1.36 × 10⁻¹⁹       |
| 5 | -0.544      | -8.71 × 10⁻²⁰       |
| ∞ | 0           | 0 (ionization)       |

The ground state energy E₁ = -13.6 eV can be derived from:

    E₁ = -mₑe⁴/(2(4πε₀)²ℏ²) = -mₑc²α²/2

where α = e²/(4πε₀ℏc) ≈ 1/137 is the fine-structure constant.

The 13.6 eV value is called the Rydberg energy:
    Ry = 13.605693 eV

IMPORTANT: The n=1 ground state has energy -13.6 eV, NOT zero! Energy is measured relative to the ionized state at E=0.

## Hydrogen Quantum Numbers

Question: What quantum numbers characterize hydrogen atom states?

Answer: Each hydrogen eigenstate is specified by three quantum numbers (n, ℓ, m):

1. Principal quantum number n = 1, 2, 3, ...
   - Determines energy: Eₙ = -13.6/n² eV
   - Determines shell: K(n=1), L(n=2), M(n=3), N(n=4)...

2. Angular momentum quantum number ℓ = 0, 1, 2, ..., n-1
   - Determines orbital angular momentum: L² = ℓ(ℓ+1)ℏ²
   - Spectroscopic notation: s(ℓ=0), p(ℓ=1), d(ℓ=2), f(ℓ=3)

3. Magnetic quantum number m = -ℓ, -ℓ+1, ..., 0, ..., ℓ-1, ℓ
   - Determines z-component: Lz = mℏ
   - Has 2ℓ+1 values for each ℓ

4. Spin quantum number ms = ±1/2
   - Electron spin: Sz = msℏ = ±ℏ/2

State naming convention: Use notation like 2p or 3d:
- First number is n
- Letter indicates ℓ

Examples:
- 1s: n=1, ℓ=0 (one state with m=0)
- 2s: n=2, ℓ=0 (one state)
- 2p: n=2, ℓ=1 (three states: m=-1,0,+1)
- 3d: n=3, ℓ=2 (five states: m=-2,-1,0,+1,+2)

Degeneracy: In hydrogen, energy depends only on n. The degeneracy of level n is:
    gₙ = 2n² (including spin)

For n=1: 2 states
For n=2: 8 states
For n=3: 18 states

## Hydrogen Wavefunctions

Question: What are the wavefunctions for hydrogen atom states?

Answer: The wavefunction separates into radial and angular parts:

    ψₙₗₘ(r,θ,φ) = Rₙₗ(r) × Yₗₘ(θ,φ)

The angular part consists of spherical harmonics Yₗₘ(θ,φ).

The radial wavefunctions are:

    Rₙₗ(r) = Nₙₗ × ρˡ × e^(-ρ/2) × L²ˡ⁺¹ₙ₋ₗ₋₁(ρ)

where:
- ρ = 2r/(na₀)
- a₀ = 4πε₀ℏ²/(mₑe²) = 0.529 Å is the Bohr radius
- L²ˡ⁺¹ₙ₋ₗ₋₁ are associated Laguerre polynomials
- Nₙₗ is a normalization constant

Explicit forms for lowest states:

1s (n=1, ℓ=0):
    ψ₁₀₀ = (1/√π)(1/a₀)³/² e^(-r/a₀)
    
The 1s orbital is spherically symmetric with most probable radius at r = a₀.

2s (n=2, ℓ=0):
    ψ₂₀₀ = (1/(4√2π))(1/a₀)³/² (2 - r/a₀) e^(-r/2a₀)
    
Has a radial node at r = 2a₀.

2p (n=2, ℓ=1, m=0):
    ψ₂₁₀ = (1/(4√2π))(1/a₀)³/² (r/a₀) e^(-r/2a₀) cos(θ)
    
This is the 2pz orbital, directed along the z-axis.

2p (n=2, ℓ=1, m=±1):
    ψ₂₁±₁ = ∓(1/(8√π))(1/a₀)³/² (r/a₀) e^(-r/2a₀) sin(θ) e^(±iφ)

Linear combinations give real 2px and 2py orbitals.

## Hydrogen Spectrum and Transitions

Question: What spectral lines does hydrogen emit and what causes them?

Answer: When an electron transitions between energy levels, a photon is emitted (or absorbed) with energy equal to the difference:

    ΔE = Eᵢ - Eᶠ = hν = hc/λ

For hydrogen:
    1/λ = R∞(1/nᶠ² - 1/nᵢ²)

where R∞ = 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹ is the Rydberg constant.

Named spectral series (nᶠ is the final state):

Lyman series (nᶠ = 1, ultraviolet):
- Lyman-α: 2→1, λ = 121.6 nm
- Lyman-β: 3→1, λ = 102.6 nm
- Series limit: ∞→1, λ = 91.2 nm

Balmer series (nᶠ = 2, visible):
- Hα: 3→2, λ = 656.3 nm (red)
- Hβ: 4→2, λ = 486.1 nm (cyan)
- Hγ: 5→2, λ = 434.0 nm (blue-violet)
- Hδ: 6→2, λ = 410.2 nm (violet)
- Series limit: ∞→2, λ = 364.6 nm

Paschen series (nᶠ = 3, infrared):
- Paschen-α: 4→3, λ = 1875 nm
- Series limit: λ = 820.4 nm

Selection rules: Not all transitions are allowed. Electric dipole transitions require:
- Δℓ = ±1 (orbital angular momentum must change by 1)
- Δm = 0, ±1 (for polarized light)

Forbidden transitions (Δℓ ≠ ±1) occur via higher multipoles (quadrupole, magnetic dipole) but are much weaker.

## The Bohr Radius and Atomic Units

Question: What is the Bohr radius and why is it significant?

Answer: The Bohr radius a₀ is the most probable distance of the electron from the nucleus in the hydrogen ground state:

    a₀ = 4πε₀ℏ²/(mₑe²) = ℏ/(mₑcα) = 5.29177 × 10⁻¹¹ m ≈ 0.529 Å

Physical significance:
- Sets the scale of atomic dimensions
- Mean radius of 1s orbital: ⟨r⟩₁ₛ = (3/2)a₀
- Most probable radius: rₘₚ = a₀
- For 2s orbital: ⟨r⟩₂ₛ = 6a₀

In atomic units, we set:
- Length: a₀ = 1
- Energy: Eₕ = e²/(4πε₀a₀) = 27.2 eV (Hartree)
- Mass: mₑ = 1
- ℏ = 1

In these units, the hydrogen energies simplify to:
    Eₙ = -1/(2n²) Hartree

The ground state energy is -0.5 Hartree = -13.6 eV.

## Fine Structure and Relativistic Corrections

Question: What are the fine structure corrections to hydrogen energy levels?

Answer: The simple Eₙ = -13.6/n² formula has small corrections from relativistic and spin effects, collectively called fine structure. These split each n level into sublevels.

The fine structure splitting is:

    ΔE_fs = Eₙ × α²/n × [n/(j+1/2) - 3/4]

where:
- α ≈ 1/137 is the fine-structure constant
- j = ℓ ± 1/2 is the total angular momentum

The correction has three physical sources:

1. Relativistic kinetic energy:
   The relativistic energy-momentum relation gives corrections of order α².

2. Spin-orbit coupling:
   The electron's spin interacts with the magnetic field from its orbital motion:
   Ĥₛₒ = (1/2mₑ²c²r)(dV/dr)L̂·Ŝ

3. Darwin term:
   A relativistic correction for s-states (ℓ=0) due to the electron's zitterbewegung.

Result for 2p level:
- 2p₃/₂ (j=3/2): higher energy
- 2p₁/₂ (j=1/2): lower energy
- Splitting: ≈ 4.5 × 10⁻⁵ eV

The Lamb shift (discovered 1947) is a smaller QED correction splitting 2s₁/₂ and 2p₁/₂, which would be degenerate in the Dirac theory. The 2s₁/₂ is about 1058 MHz (4.37 × 10⁻⁶ eV) higher than 2p₁/₂.

## Hydrogen-like Atoms

Question: How do hydrogen energy levels scale with nuclear charge Z?

Answer: For hydrogen-like ions (one electron, nuclear charge Ze), the Coulomb potential becomes:

    V(r) = -Ze²/(4πε₀r)

The energy levels scale as Z²:

    Eₙ = -Z² × 13.6 eV / n²

The Bohr radius scales as 1/Z:

    aₙ = a₀ × n²/Z

Examples:

He⁺ (Z=2):
- E₁ = -4 × 13.6 = -54.4 eV
- E₂ = -4 × 3.4 = -13.6 eV

Li²⁺ (Z=3):
- E₁ = -9 × 13.6 = -122.4 eV

Be³⁺ (Z=4):
- E₁ = -16 × 13.6 = -217.6 eV

The ionization energy of hydrogen-like ions follows:
    I.E. = Z² × 13.6 eV

For heavy ions, relativistic corrections become important and scale as (Zα)², where effects become significant when Zα approaches 1 (Z ≈ 137).

## Quantum Superposition

Question: What is quantum superposition and how is it described mathematically?

Answer: Quantum superposition is the principle that a quantum system can exist in multiple states simultaneously until measured. This is not uncertainty about which state the system is in—it is genuinely in all states at once.

Mathematical formulation: If |ψ₁⟩ and |ψ₂⟩ are valid quantum states, then any linear combination is also a valid state:

    |ψ⟩ = α|ψ₁⟩ + β|ψ₂⟩

where α and β are complex numbers (amplitudes) satisfying:
    |α|² + |β|² = 1 (normalization)

For a qubit (two-level system), the general state is:

    |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ = cos(θ/2)|0⟩ + e^(iφ)sin(θ/2)|1⟩

This maps to a point on the Bloch sphere with polar angle θ and azimuthal angle φ.

Key properties of superposition:

1. Measurement collapses the superposition:
   - Probability of measuring |0⟩: P(0) = |α|²
   - Probability of measuring |1⟩: P(1) = |β|²
   - After measurement, the state is either |0⟩ or |1⟩

2. Interference: Superpositions exhibit interference effects. For state |ψ⟩ = (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2 evolved through a Hadamard gate:
   - The amplitudes can add (constructive interference)
   - Or cancel (destructive interference)

3. No classical analog: A classical bit is either 0 or 1. A qubit in superposition is both simultaneously.

Example: The spin state of a spin-1/2 particle along x:
    |+x⟩ = (|↑z⟩ + |↓z⟩)/√2
    |-x⟩ = (|↑z⟩ - |↓z⟩)/√2

If we measure Sz on |+x⟩, we get +ℏ/2 or -ℏ/2 with 50% probability each.

## The Dirac Bra-Ket Notation

Question: What is bra-ket notation and how do we use it?

Answer: Dirac's bra-ket notation provides a powerful, basis-independent way to describe quantum states and operators.

Kets (|⟩): Column vectors representing quantum states
    |ψ⟩ = (α, β)ᵀ

Bras (⟨|): Row vectors, the conjugate transpose of kets
    ⟨ψ| = (α*, β*)

Inner product (bra-ket):
    ⟨φ|ψ⟩ = Σᵢ φᵢ*ψᵢ = complex number

Properties:
- ⟨ψ|ψ⟩ = 1 for normalized states
- ⟨φ|ψ⟩ = ⟨ψ|φ⟩*
- |⟨φ|ψ⟩|² is the probability that |ψ⟩ is found in state |φ⟩

Outer product (ket-bra):
    |ψ⟩⟨φ| = matrix (operator)

The projection operator onto state |n⟩ is:
    P̂ₙ = |n⟩⟨n|

Completeness relation (resolution of identity):
    Σₙ |n⟩⟨n| = Î

For continuous variables:
    ∫ |x⟩⟨x| dx = Î

Operators in bra-ket notation:
    ⟨φ|Â|ψ⟩ = matrix element
    ⟨n|Â|n⟩ = expectation value in state |n⟩

Hermitian operators satisfy Â† = Â:
    ⟨φ|Â|ψ⟩ = ⟨ψ|Â|φ⟩*

## Quantum Entanglement

Question: What is quantum entanglement and why can't it be explained classically?

Answer: Entanglement is a quantum correlation between particles that cannot be described by classical physics or by treating particles independently. When particles are entangled, the state of the combined system cannot be written as a product of individual states.

Separable (not entangled) state:
    |ψ⟩_sep = |φ⟩_A ⊗ |χ⟩_B

Entangled state (example: Bell state Φ⁺):
    |Φ⁺⟩ = (1/√2)(|00⟩ + |11⟩)

This CANNOT be written as |φ⟩_A ⊗ |χ⟩_B for any choice of |φ⟩ and |χ⟩.

The four Bell states form a maximally entangled basis:
    |Φ⁺⟩ = (1/√2)(|00⟩ + |11⟩)
    |Φ⁻⟩ = (1/√2)(|00⟩ - |11⟩)
    |Ψ⁺⟩ = (1/√2)(|01⟩ + |10⟩)
    |Ψ⁻⟩ = (1/√2)(|01⟩ - |10⟩)

Properties of entanglement:

1. Perfect correlations: In |Φ⁺⟩, if Alice measures |0⟩, Bob always gets |0⟩. If Alice measures |1⟩, Bob always gets |1⟩.

2. Non-local correlations: The correlation persists regardless of spatial separation. Measuring Alice's particle instantly determines Bob's, even light-years away.

3. No signaling: Despite correlations, entanglement cannot transmit information faster than light. Alice cannot control her measurement outcome.

4. Monogamy: A qubit maximally entangled with one particle cannot be entangled with another.

## Bell's Theorem and Bell Inequalities

Question: What is Bell's theorem and what do Bell inequalities prove?

Answer: Bell's theorem (1964) proves that no theory of local hidden variables can reproduce all predictions of quantum mechanics. This demonstrates that quantum correlations are fundamentally non-classical.

The CHSH inequality (most commonly tested form):

    |⟨AB⟩ + ⟨AB'⟩ + ⟨A'B⟩ - ⟨A'B'⟩| ≤ 2

where A, A' are Alice's measurements and B, B' are Bob's.

Local hidden variable theories satisfy this bound. Quantum mechanics predicts:

For the Bell state |Φ⁺⟩ with optimal measurement angles:
    S = 2√2 ≈ 2.83

This violates the inequality (2.83 > 2), proving:
1. Nature is not locally realistic
2. Either locality or realism (or both) must be abandoned
3. Quantum mechanics correctly describes this non-classical behavior

Experimental tests (Aspect 1982, Zeilinger 2015, Nobel Prize 2022):
- Consistently measure S ≈ 2.7-2.8
- Loopholes (locality, detection) have been closed
- Bell inequality violations are a robust feature of nature

Implications:
- Hidden variable theories (like Bohm's) must be non-local
- Quantum correlations are "stronger than classical but weaker than signaling"
- Foundation for quantum cryptography security proofs

## Density Matrices and Mixed States

Question: What is the density matrix and when do we need it?

Answer: The density matrix (density operator) ρ generalizes pure state quantum mechanics to handle:
1. Statistical mixtures of states (classical uncertainty)
2. Subsystems of entangled states (partial trace)

For a pure state |ψ⟩:
    ρ = |ψ⟩⟨ψ|

Properties:
- Tr(ρ) = 1 (normalization)
- ρ† = ρ (Hermitian)
- ρ ≥ 0 (positive semi-definite)
- Tr(ρ²) = 1 for pure states, Tr(ρ²) < 1 for mixed states

For a statistical mixture with probabilities pᵢ and states |ψᵢ⟩:
    ρ = Σᵢ pᵢ |ψᵢ⟩⟨ψᵢ|

Example: 50% |0⟩ and 50% |1⟩ (mixed state):
    ρ = (1/2)|0⟩⟨0| + (1/2)|1⟩⟨1| = (1/2)I

This is NOT the same as the superposition (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2!

The superposition has:
    ρ_super = (1/2)(|0⟩⟨0| + |0⟩⟨1| + |1⟩⟨0| + |1⟩⟨1|)

The off-diagonal terms (coherences) are present in superpositions but absent in mixtures.

Expectation values:
    ⟨Â⟩ = Tr(ρÂ)

Time evolution:
    iℏ dρ/dt = [Ĥ, ρ] (von Neumann equation)

Reduced density matrix: For a bipartite system AB, the state of A alone is:
    ρ_A = Tr_B(ρ_AB)

For the Bell state |Φ⁺⟩:
    ρ_A = (1/2)|0⟩⟨0| + (1/2)|1⟩⟨1| = I/2

The subsystem appears maximally mixed even though the total state is pure!

## Von Neumann Entropy

Question: How do we quantify quantum uncertainty and entanglement?

Answer: The von Neumann entropy generalizes classical Shannon entropy to quantum systems:

    S(ρ) = -Tr(ρ log ρ) = -Σᵢ λᵢ log λᵢ

where λᵢ are the eigenvalues of ρ (log is typically base 2 for bits or natural log for nats).

Properties:
- S(ρ) = 0 for pure states (no uncertainty)
- S(ρ) = log(d) for maximally mixed state I/d in d dimensions
- S(ρ) is concave: S(Σpᵢρᵢ) ≥ ΣpᵢS(ρᵢ)

For a qubit:
- Pure state: S = 0 bits
- Maximally mixed: S = 1 bit

Entanglement entropy: For a bipartite pure state |ψ⟩_AB, the entanglement is measured by:
    E(|ψ⟩) = S(ρ_A) = S(ρ_B)

For Bell states:
    S(ρ_A) = log(2) = 1 bit (maximally entangled)

For separable states:
    S(ρ_A) = 0 (no entanglement)

Other entanglement measures:
- Entanglement of formation
- Concurrence: C = max(0, λ₁ - λ₂ - λ₃ - λ₄)
- Negativity: N = (||ρ^{T_A}||₁ - 1)/2

The relationship: For two qubits:
    E = h((1 + √(1-C²))/2)
where h is the binary entropy.

## The Schmidt Decomposition

Question: What is the Schmidt decomposition and why is it useful?

Answer: Any bipartite pure state can be written in a special diagonal form called the Schmidt decomposition:

    |ψ⟩_AB = Σᵢ λᵢ |αᵢ⟩_A |βᵢ⟩_B

where:
- λᵢ ≥ 0 are Schmidt coefficients with Σλᵢ² = 1
- |αᵢ⟩ are orthonormal in A
- |βᵢ⟩ are orthonormal in B
- The number of non-zero λᵢ is the Schmidt rank

Properties:
1. The reduced density matrices have the same non-zero eigenvalues:
   ρ_A = Σᵢ λᵢ² |αᵢ⟩⟨αᵢ|
   ρ_B = Σᵢ λᵢ² |βᵢ⟩⟨βᵢ|

2. Schmidt rank = 1 ⟺ state is separable (not entangled)

3. Equal Schmidt coefficients ⟺ maximally entangled

Example: Bell state |Φ⁺⟩
    |Φ⁺⟩ = (1/√2)|0⟩|0⟩ + (1/√2)|1⟩|1⟩

Already in Schmidt form with λ₁ = λ₂ = 1/√2 (maximally entangled).

Example: Product state |0⟩|1⟩
Has Schmidt rank 1 (λ₁ = 1, all others 0), confirming no entanglement.

The Schmidt decomposition is found by SVD of the coefficient matrix:
If |ψ⟩ = Σᵢⱼ cᵢⱼ|i⟩|j⟩, then C = UΛV† gives the decomposition.

## No-Cloning Theorem

Question: Why can't we copy unknown quantum states?

Answer: The no-cloning theorem (Wootters-Zurek 1982) proves that it is impossible to create an exact copy of an arbitrary unknown quantum state. This is a fundamental difference from classical information.

Statement: There is no unitary operator U such that for all |ψ⟩:
    U|ψ⟩|0⟩ = |ψ⟩|ψ⟩

Proof by contradiction:
Suppose such U exists. For two different states |ψ⟩ and |φ⟩:
    U|ψ⟩|0⟩ = |ψ⟩|ψ⟩
    U|φ⟩|0⟩ = |φ⟩|φ⟩

Taking the inner product:
    ⟨ψ|φ⟩⟨0|0⟩ = ⟨ψ|φ⟩²
    ⟨ψ|φ⟩ = ⟨ψ|φ⟩²

This equation has solutions only for ⟨ψ|φ⟩ = 0 or 1, meaning |ψ⟩ and |φ⟩ must be identical or orthogonal. A universal cloner cannot exist.

Implications:
1. Quantum cryptography security: Eavesdroppers cannot copy qubits without detection
2. No faster-than-light signaling via entanglement
3. Quantum error correction must use redundancy, not copying
4. Quantum money and quantum software protection are possible

What IS possible:
- Cloning orthogonal states (if you know which they are)
- Approximate cloning (with fidelity < 1)
- Classical information can be copied (after measurement)
- Teleportation transfers state but destroys original

The optimal approximate cloner achieves fidelity:
    F = (d+1)/(2d+2) for d-dimensional systems
    F = 5/6 ≈ 0.833 for qubits
# SECTION 4: ANGULAR MOMENTUM AND SPIN
# Detailed treatment of quantized rotation and intrinsic angular momentum

## Orbital Angular Momentum

Question: How is angular momentum described in quantum mechanics?

Answer: Angular momentum is quantized in discrete units of ℏ. The orbital angular momentum operator is L̂ = r̂ × p̂.

The magnitude squared L̂² and the z-component L̂z commute:
    [L̂², L̂z] = 0

This allows them to have simultaneous eigenstates |ℓ,m⟩ (spherical harmonics Yₗₘ):

    L̂²|ℓ,m⟩ = ℓ(ℓ+1)ℏ² |ℓ,m⟩
    L̂z|ℓ,m⟩ = mℏ |ℓ,m⟩

Quantum numbers:
- ℓ = 0, 1, 2, ... (orbital quantum number)
- m = -ℓ, ..., +ℓ (magnetic quantum number)

The x and y components do NOT commute with L̂z:
    [L̂x, L̂y] = iℏL̂z
    [L̂y, L̂z] = iℏL̂x
    [L̂z, L̂x] = iℏL̂y

This means we cannot simultaneously know all three components of angular momentum. We can only know the magnitude (ℓ) and one component (usually z).

Ladder operators L̂± = L̂x ± iL̂y allow moving between m states:
    L̂±|ℓ,m⟩ = ℏ√(ℓ(ℓ+1) - m(m±1)) |ℓ,m±1⟩

---

## Intrinsic Spin

Question: What is spin and how does it differ from orbital angular momentum?

Answer: Spin is an intrinsic form of angular momentum carried by elementary particles, with no classical analog. It is not due to physical rotation but is a fundamental property like mass or charge.

Spin operators Ŝ satisfy the same commutation relations as L̂:
    [Ŝx, Ŝy] = iℏŜz

Eigenvalue equations:
    Ŝ²|s,ms⟩ = s(s+1)ℏ² |s,ms⟩
    Ŝz|s,ms⟩ = msℏ |s,ms⟩

Crucial difference: The spin quantum number s can be half-integer!
- Electrons, protons, neutrons: s = 1/2 (Fermions)
- Photons: s = 1 (Bosons)
- Higgs boson: s = 0
- Graviton (hypothetical): s = 2

For a spin-1/2 particle (electron):
- s = 1/2
- ms = +1/2 (spin up, |↑⟩) or -1/2 (spin down, |↓⟩)

The magnitude of electron spin is fixed:
    S = ℏ√(3/4) ≈ 0.866 ℏ

The z-component is ±ℏ/2.

---

## Pauli Matrices

Question: How do we represent spin-1/2 mathematically?

Answer: For spin-1/2 particles, the operators are represented by 2x2 matrices called Pauli matrices (σ).

    Ŝ = (ℏ/2)σ

The Pauli matrices are:

    σx = (0  1)
         (1  0)

    σy = (0 -i)
         (i  0)

    σz = (1  0)
         (0 -1)

Properties:
- σᵢ² = I (Identity)
- σxσy = iσz (cyclic)
- Tr(σᵢ) = 0
- det(σᵢ) = -1
- Eigenvalues are ±1

The general state of a qubit (spin-1/2) is a spinor:
    |ψ⟩ = (α)
          (β)

The expectation value of spin in direction n̂ is:
    ⟨S·n̂⟩ = (ℏ/2) ⟨ψ|σ·n̂|ψ⟩

## Stern-Gerlach Experiment

Question: What does the Stern-Gerlach experiment prove?

Answer: The Stern-Gerlach experiment (1922) demonstrated the quantization of angular momentum. Silver atoms (which have one unpaired electron, effectively spin-1/2) were sent through an inhomogeneous magnetic field.

Classical prediction: The magnetic moment vectors are randomly oriented, so the beam should spread into a continuous smear.

Quantum result: The beam split into exactly two discrete components (deflected up and down).

Significance:
1. Proved that spatial quantization exists (only discrete values of Lz or Sz are allowed).
2. For silver atoms in an s-state (L=0), the splitting must be due to intrinsic angular momentum (spin).
3. Demonstrated that measurement collapses the state into an eigenstate of the measurement operator.

The force on the dipole is Fz = μz (dB/dz). Since μz is proportional to Sz, the force is quantized:
    Fz ∝ ±ℏ/2

---

## Addition of Angular Momentum

Question: How do we combine angular momenta of multiple particles?

Answer: When two particles with angular momenta j₁ and j₂ interact, the total angular momentum J = J₁ + J₂ is conserved.

The allowed values for the total quantum number j are:
    |j₁ - j₂| ≤ j ≤ j₁ + j₂
    in integer steps.

Example: Two spin-1/2 particles (s₁=1/2, s₂=1/2)
Possible total spin s:
- s = 1/2 + 1/2 = 1 (Triplet state, 3 components)
- s = 1/2 - 1/2 = 0 (Singlet state, 1 component)

The coupled basis states |j, m_j⟩ are related to the uncoupled basis |j₁, m₁⟩|j₂, m₂⟩ by Clebsch-Gordan coefficients:

    |j, m_j⟩ = Σ C(j₁j₂j; m₁m₂m_j) |j₁, m₁⟩|j₂, m₂⟩

Example: Singlet state (s=0, ms=0)
    |0,0⟩ = (1/√2)(|↑↓⟩ - |↓↑⟩)

Example: Triplet states (s=1)
    |1,1⟩ = |↑↑⟩
    |1,0⟩ = (1/√2)(|↑↓⟩ + |↓↑⟩)
    |1,-1⟩ = |↓↓⟩

This explains why the ground state of Helium (two electrons) is a singlet (spins paired, antisymmetric spatial part) and excited states can be triplets.
# SECTION 5: ADVANCED QUANTUM PHENOMENA
# Tunneling, Perturbation Theory, and Decoherence

## Quantum Tunneling

Question: What is quantum tunneling and how is the transmission probability calculated?

Answer: Quantum tunneling is a phenomenon where a particle passes through a potential barrier that it classically could not surmount because its total energy E is less than the barrier height V₀.

For a rectangular barrier of height V₀ and width a:
Region I (x<0): Free particle
Region II (0<x<a): V = V₀ > E
Region III (x>a): Free particle

In the barrier region, the wavefunction is exponential rather than oscillatory:
    ψ(x) = C e^(-κx) + D e^(κx)

where κ = √(2m(V₀ - E))/ℏ is the decay constant.

The transmission probability T (for a "thick" barrier κa >> 1) is approximately:

    T ≈ 16 (E/V₀) (1 - E/V₀) e^(-2κa)

The dominant factor is the exponential term:
    T ∝ e^(-2κa) = exp(-2a√(2m(V₀-E))/ℏ)

Applications:
1. Alpha decay: Alpha particles tunnel out of the nucleus.
2. Scanning Tunneling Microscope (STM): Electrons tunnel between tip and surface.
3. Nuclear fusion in stars: Protons tunnel through Coulomb repulsion.
4. Tunnel diodes and Josephson junctions.

---

## Perturbation Theory

Question: How do we approximate solutions for complex quantum systems?

Answer: Time-independent perturbation theory allows finding approximate eigenstates and eigenvalues for a Hamiltonian Ĥ = Ĥ₀ + λĤ', where Ĥ₀ is exactly solvable and λĤ' is a small perturbation.

First-order energy correction:
    Eₙ⁽¹⁾ = ⟨n⁰|Ĥ'|n⁰⟩

This is simply the expectation value of the perturbation in the unperturbed state.

Second-order energy correction:
    Eₙ⁽²⁾ = Σₘ≠ₙ |⟨m⁰|Ĥ'|n⁰⟩|² / (Eₙ⁰ - Eₘ⁰)

This describes the mixing of states. Note that levels repel: the ground state energy is always lowered by second-order perturbation.

First-order wavefunction correction:
    |n⁽¹⁾⟩ = Σₘ≠ₙ (⟨m⁰|Ĥ'|n⁰⟩ / (Eₙ⁰ - Eₘ⁰)) |m⁰⟩

Example: Stark effect (atom in electric field).
- First order correction is zero for ground state (parity symmetry).
- Second order gives quadratic shift E ∝ -αE_field².

## Fermi's Golden Rule

Question: How do we calculate transition rates between quantum states?

Answer: Fermi's Golden Rule gives the transition rate Γ (probability per unit time) from an initial eigenstate |i⟩ to a continuum of final states |f⟩ under a time-dependent perturbation Ĥ'(t).

    Γ_{i→f} = (2π/ℏ) |⟨f|Ĥ'|i⟩|² ρ(E_f)

where:
- |⟨f|Ĥ'|i⟩|² is the squared matrix element of the perturbation
- ρ(E_f) is the density of states at the final energy

This formula is derived from time-dependent perturbation theory in the long-time limit.

Applications:
1. Spontaneous emission rates (atomic lifetimes)
2. Scattering cross-sections (Born approximation)
3. Beta decay rates
4. Absorption and stimulated emission coefficients

## Quantum Decoherence

Question: How does the classical world emerge from quantum mechanics?

Answer: Quantum decoherence is the process by which a quantum system loses its quantum coherence (superposition capabilities) due to interaction with the environment. It explains the "appearance" of wavefunction collapse without requiring a separate postulate.

Mechanism:
1. System S interacts with Environment E.
2. Initial state: |ψ⟩_S ⊗ |0⟩_E = (α|0⟩ + β|1⟩) ⊗ |0⟩_E
3. Time evolution entangles them:
   → α|0⟩_S|e₀⟩_E + β|1⟩_S|e₁⟩_E
   where |e₀⟩ and |e₁⟩ are orthogonal environment states.

4. The reduced density matrix of the system becomes:
   ρ_S = Tr_E(ρ_total) = |α|²|0⟩⟨0| + |β|²|1⟩⟨1|

The off-diagonal terms (coherences) αβ*⟨e₁|e₀⟩ vanish because ⟨e₁|e₀⟩ → 0 for a large environment.

Result: The system behaves like a classical statistical mixture, not a quantum superposition. The "cat" is either dead or alive, not both.

Timescale: Decoherence happens extremely fast for macroscopic objects.
- Dust particle in air: Decoherence time ≈ 10⁻³¹ s
- Electron in vacuum: Can maintain coherence for seconds

This is why we don't see quantum superpositions in everyday life and why building quantum computers is difficult (they must be isolated from decoherence).

## The Quantum Zeno Effect

Question: Can observation stop a quantum system from changing?

Answer: The Quantum Zeno Effect is the suppression of unitary time evolution by frequent measurements.

If a system is in state |ψ₀⟩ and we measure it N times in time T (interval Δt = T/N):
- Probability of survival after one interval: P ≈ 1 - (Δt/τ_Z)²
- Probability after N intervals: P_total ≈ [1 - (T/Nτ_Z)²]^N
- Limit as N → ∞: P_total → 1

Continuous observation freezes the system in its initial state.

Inverse Zeno Effect: Frequent measurements can also accelerate decay or guide the system into a specific state (quantum control).
